About the Library.

The Internet Public Library is a free, web-based research and reference environment which displays information from web libraries and datasets through a science-fiction inspired interface. It can be used for discovery, learning, or visiting a certain era of something. Whether an artist is looking for visual references or whether someone is curious about what a printing of something looked like in the 70's, anyone can benefit from the presence of record meta-data, which offers context and sources to the objects and stories being viewed. Visual reference with data rather than without makes a great difference in the potential for a richer learning experience that would otherwise not be there if one were to browse for images in a content aggregation site where original data and sources are not cited.

Datasets come from a range of sources including public APIs, cultural institutions, and community-maintained wikis. The system ingests heterogeneous data, organizes it, and renders it through a single page application. Each dataset or in-house application is its own engine. Any applicable filters and navigations are unique to each implementation while still having overall user-interface similiarity across the site, letting heterogenous data in abundance and variance become accessible with familiarity. Scouting for new dataset implementations happens regularly during development. There are no restrictions on subject matter.

Whether the data is open or gray-area, is updating or is a static archive, keeping data live on the library is a hope for digital media preservation. We will lose more of our history if at least some of us do not act now. Computer graphics of the 90's could have been an era of documented aesthetics, but is instead ephemerally lost because we simply did not consider preservation.

In finality, the library's handling of data is responsible for:

  • Respectfully caring for cultural history through digital stewardship.
  • Formatting information for viewing that is most data-immediate and comfortable.
  • Make each dataset or in-house application have greater potential as a resource for learning under any use case.
  • Updating the library's collection over time with new datasets.
  • Having everything available for anyone to access so that the power of knowledge is shared at scale.
