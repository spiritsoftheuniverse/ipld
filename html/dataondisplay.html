More Data At Once

The library organizes each implementation's navigation options in the user-interface as a map to everything. Table of Contents, content filters, and category links all assist in guiding the user through the whole dataset.

Query results are optimized for data-immediacy, exposing all data directly within the results page. This eliminates back-and-forth page traversal by eliminating the full-details page.

The exposure of full-data on every record in view is as functional as it is aesthetic. The content volume occupies comfortable amounts of space, while the data is made readably dense, like that of Japanese product packaging.

Full-data output runs the risk of data-overabundance. Vertical spacing is maintained with various content collapsing methods. Long text is collapsed into description containers with show-more toggles. Panels are tabbed in order to alter context within the same space, which both introduces and relieves layout shifting.

Pagination

The library uses offset pagination for smaller datasets. For deep paging, both keyset and offset pagination are used together. The page-limit is curated for each implementation based on the average html output length of a query. Art museums mostly show 20 records per query due to their meta-data and thumbnail richness. Implementations with less meta-data output will show a higher number of records.

Lazy Loading
Lazy loading of images at the library is primarily for respecting online traffic. It was forced into creation when viewing The Smithsonian Field Book Project, which is a department from The Smithsonian that has dozens of thumbnails per record. Even with a 20 record limit per page, the amount of thumbnails forced rate-limiting from the image server (IDS) against the IP-address in which data was being received. Deferring the loading based on viewing proximity solved the issue and lazy loading has since been implemented into nearly every dataset.
Earliest Filter
Commercial data from the likes of AniList (anime data) and IGDB (video games data) at their establishments usually reinforce new or upcoming records. The library is concerned with that as well as historical records. There are usually always filters for popular, latest, or upcoming, although there is never one for earliest records. The earliest filter transforms a dataset from one which caters to consumers, into one that is an act of stewardship for the sake of onlookers viewing information under any context. It is critical despite being so innocuous.
Trusting People / No Analytics

The library is developed for both progression of function and aesthetic. The library trusts that people will be able to benefit from such progression. This is generally not the case for how the business world handles its customers, where they attempt to get a grip on perceived familiarity through metrics. The library gathers data for people, not on people, therefore all brain-power and effort is kept in focus. Scripts such as Google Analytics are not acceptable.

Data-immediacy and design with graphical personality are but a few things that break tradition at the library. It however, does not go out of the way to be different. Foundational design is still respected, and pushing for utmost-simplicity is immutable. Its the little things that need change, and rather than highlighting such changes publicly as if it were a shiny feature to look forward to or turn away out of fear or curiosity, everything is just there. Nobody needs to know that full-view page design was eliminated so that it streamlines the browsing experience by x, where x is the number of records in the query. It only needs to be an experience that is developed because people are definitely more capable of taking on more than what metrics give them credit for. Everything in the world suffers through this modern-day, metric induced software development environment for this reason, and the library does not follow suit.
